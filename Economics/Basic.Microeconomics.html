Microeconomics Notes

Introduction to Microeconomics

  • Definition and Scope
    • Definition: Study of individual economic units (consumers, firms, markets).
    • Scope: Consumer behavior, firm theory, market structures, resource allocation.
    • Extensions: Game theory, information economics, behavioral economics.
  • Importance
    • Behavioral Insights: Reveals patterns in consumer preferences and firm strategies.
    • Policy Applications: Informs regulatory decisions and market interventions.
  • Methodology
    • Positive vs. Normative Analysis:
      • Positive: What is (factual analysis).
      • Normative: What ought to be (value judgments).
    • Models and Assumptions:
      • Simplification of complex realities to derive insights.
      • Ceteris paribus assumption: "all else being equal".
  • Key Principles
    • Scarcity and Choice: Limited resources necessitate trade-offs.
    • Incentives: Economic agents respond to incentives.
    • Opportunity Cost: The next best alternative forgone.

Demand and Supply Analysis

  • Demand
    • Law of Demand: Inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.
    • Determinants:
      • Price: Primary factor in the demand function.
      • Income: Normal vs. inferior goods.
      • Tastes and Preferences: Influenced by social, cultural factors.
      • Expectations: Anticipated future prices or income changes.
      • Substitutes and Complements: Cross-price effects.
    • Individual vs. Market Demand: Aggregation of individual demand curves.
  • Supply
    • Law of Supply: Direct relationship between price and quantity supplied.
    • Determinants:
      • Production Cost: Input prices, technology.
      • Technology: Efficiency improvements shift supply curve.
      • Expectations: Anticipated market conditions.
      • Number of Sellers: Market entry/exit effects.
    • Individual vs. Market Supply: Aggregation of individual firm supply.
  • Market Equilibrium
    • Equilibrium Price and Quantity: Intersection of supply and demand curves.
    • Shifts and Movements:
      • Movement along curves: Price changes.
      • Shifts of curves: Changes in non-price determinants.
    • Comparative Statics: Analysis of equilibrium changes due to parameter shifts.
    • Disequilibrium and Adjustment:
      • Surplus: Excess supply at a given price.
      • Shortage: Excess demand at a given price.
  • Dynamic Analysis
    • Cobweb Model: Explains price fluctuations in markets with production lags.
    • Expectations and Speculation: Role in shaping market dynamics.

Elasticity

  • Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)
    • Definition: Measure of responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes.
    • Formula: % change in quantity demanded / % change in price.
    • Types:
      • Elastic (|PED| > 1): Quantity change > Price change.
      • Inelastic (|PED| < 1): Quantity change < Price change.
      • Unit Elastic (|PED| = 1): Quantity change = Price change.
    • Determinants:
      • Availability of substitutes.
      • Necessity vs. luxury goods.
      • Time horizon (short-run vs. long-run elasticity).
    • Applications:
      • Pricing strategies for firms.
      • Tax incidence analysis.
  • Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)
    • Definition: Responsiveness of quantity supplied to price changes.
    • Formula: % change in quantity supplied / % change in price.
    • Determinants:
      • Time period for production adjustment.
      • Capacity utilization and scalability.
      • Resource mobility and availability.
    • Applications:
      • Production planning.
      • Supply chain management.
  • Cross-Price Elasticity
    • Definition: Effect of price change in one good on quantity demanded of another.
    • Formula: % change in quantity demanded of good A / % change in price of good B.
    • Interpretation:
      • Positive: Substitutes.
      • Negative: Complements.
      • Zero: Independent goods.
    • Applications:
      • Product positioning and marketing strategies.
      • Competitive analysis.
  • Income Elasticity of Demand
    • Definition: Responsiveness of demand to changes in consumer income.
    • Formula: % change in quantity demanded / % change in income.
    • Types:
      • Normal Goods (Positive elasticity).
      • Inferior Goods (Negative elasticity).
      • Luxury vs. Necessity Goods.
    • Applications:
      • Market segmentation.
      • Economic development analysis.
  • Arc vs. Point Elasticity
    • Point Elasticity: Elasticity at a specific point on the demand/supply curve.
    • Arc Elasticity: Average elasticity over a range of the curve.
  • Advanced Elasticity Concepts
    • Constant Elasticity Functions: Mathematical properties and applications.
    • Elasticity in Calculus: Derivative-based formulations.
    • Cross-Elasticities in Demand Systems: Slutsky equation and demand interdependencies.

Consumer Theory

  • Utility
    • Concepts:
      • Cardinal vs. Ordinal Utility.
      • Total Utility vs. Marginal Utility.
    • Utility Functions:
      • Mathematical representations of preferences.
      • Common forms: Cobb-Douglas, CES (Constant Elasticity of Substitution).
    • Marginal Utility:
      • Additional satisfaction from consuming an extra unit.
      • Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility.
  • Preferences and Indifference Curves
    • Axioms of Rational Choice:
      • Completeness, Transitivity, Continuity, Non-satiation.
    • Indifference Curves:
      • Definition: Combinations of goods providing equal satisfaction.
      • Properties: Downward sloping, convex to origin.
    • Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS):
      • Rate at which a consumer is willing to substitute one good for another.
      • Relationship to marginal utility.
  • Budget Constraint
    • Definition: Limit on consumption choices based on income and prices.
    • Mathematical Representation:
      • Linear equation in two-good model.
      • Extensions to multiple goods.
    • Effects of Income and Price Changes:
      • Shifts and rotations of the budget line.
  • Consumer Choice and Optimization
    • Utility Maximization:
      • Tangency condition: MRS = Price Ratio.
      • Lagrangian method for constrained optimization.
    • Corner Solutions:
      • When tangency conditions don't hold.
    • Applications:
      • Deriving individual demand curves.
      • Engel curves and income consumption curves.
  • Income and Substitution Effects
    • Slutsky Equation:
      • Decomposing price effects into income and substitution components.
    • Hicks vs. Slutsky Decomposition:
      • Different approaches to isolating effects.
    • Applications:
      • Giffen goods phenomenon.
      • Labor supply decisions (backward-bending supply curveistical vs. taste-based discrimination.
      • Wage gaps and their explanations.
    • Unions and Collective Bargaining:
      • Effects on wages and employment.
      • Bilateral monopoly model.
  • Capital Markets
    • Types of Capital:
      • Physical capital vs. financial capital.
    • Investment Decisions:
      • Net Present Value (NPV) criterion.
      • Internal Rate of Return (IRR).
    • Cost of Capital:
      • Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).
      • Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM).
    • Efficient Market Hypothesis:
      • Weak, semi-strong, and strong forms.
    • Market for Loanable Funds:
      • Equilibrium interest rate determination.
      • Impact of government borrowing (crowding out).
  • Land and Natural Resources
    • Economic Rent:
      • Ricardian theory of rent.
    • Resource Extraction:
      • Hotelling's rule for non-renewable resources.
    • Sustainability:
      • Renewable vs. non-renewable resources.
      • Tragedy of the commons.
  • Global Labor Markets
    • Outsourcing and Offshoring:
      • Cost-benefit analysis for firms.
      • Impact on domestic and foreign labor markets.
    • Immigration and Labor Mobility:
      • Effects on wage rates and employment.
      • Skill-biased technological change.

Advanced Game Theory

  • Dynamic Games
    • Definition: Games where players move sequentially.
    • Extensive Form Representation: Tree diagrams showing the sequence of moves.
    • Subgame Perfect Equilibrium: Refinement of Nash equilibrium for dynamic games.
    • Applications:
      • Entry deterrence strategies in business.
      • Sequential bargaining in negotiations.
  • Bayesian Games
    • Concept: Games with incomplete information.
    • Harsanyi Transformation: Converting games of incomplete information to games of imperfect information.
    • Bayesian Nash Equilibrium: Equilibrium concept for games of incomplete information.
    • Applications:
      • Auctions with private valuations.
      • Signaling games in labor markets and education.
  • Business Strategy Applications
    • Commitment Strategies: Using irreversible investments to deter entry.
    • Reputation Building: Repeated games and the importance of reputation in business.
    • Coopetition: Simultaneous competition and cooperation in oligopolistic markets.

Information Economics

  • Adverse Selection
    • Definition: Market inefficiency due to information asymmetry before a transaction.
    • Examples: Used car market (lemons problem), insurance markets.
    • Solutions: Screening, signaling, market design.
    • Business Applications:
      • Employee hiring processes.
      • Credit market lending practices.
  • Moral Hazard
    • Concept: Changes in behavior when individuals are protected from risk.
    • Principal-Agent Problem: Misaligned incentives between principals and agents.
    • Solutions: Incentive contracts, monitoring.
    • Business Applications:
      • Executive compensation design.
      • Insurance policy structuring.
  • Signaling
    • Theory: Actions taken by informed parties to credibly convey information.
    • Spence Signaling Model: Education as a signal in labor markets.
    • Business Applications:
      • Brand building and reputation management.
      • Corporate financial decisions as signals to investors.
  • Screening
    • Concept: Uninformed parties designing mechanisms to elicit information.
    • Self-Selection: Offering a menu of options to induce revelation of private information.
    • Business Applications:
      • Product line design for price discrimination.
      • Job candidate assessment techniques.

Behavioral Economics

  • Prospect Theory
    • Key Concepts:
      • Reference dependence: Outcomes evaluated relative to a reference point.
      • Loss aversion: Losses loom larger than equivalent gains.
      • Diminishing sensitivity: Decreasing marginal impact of gains and losses.
    • Value Function: S-shaped curve capturing risk attitudes.
    • Probability Weighting: Overweighting of small probabilities, underweighting of large probabilities.
    • Business Applications:
      • Framing of product offerings and pricing strategies.
      • Understanding consumer decision-making under uncertainty.
  • Mental Accounting
    • Definition: Cognitive operations used by individuals to organize, evaluate, and keep track of financial activities.
    • Key Aspects:
      • Categorization of expenditures.
      • Treatment of opportunity costs.
      • Sunk cost fallacy.
    • Business Applications:
      • Designing loyalty programs and bundled offerings.
      • Structuring financial products and payment plans.
  • Behavioral Finance
    • Market Anomalies: Patterns in financial markets inconsistent with efficient market hypothesis.
    • Investor Biases: Overconfidence, herding behavior, disposition effect.
    • Business Applications:
      • Designing investment products and services.
      • Corporate finance decisions considering behavioral factors.

Advanced Industrial Organization

  • Two-Sided Markets
    • Definition: Markets where platforms facilitate interactions between two distinct user groups.
    • Network Effects: Cross-side and same-side externalities.
    • Pricing Strategies: Balancing act to get both sides on board.
    • Business Examples: Credit card markets, operating systems, social media platforms.
  • Network Effects
    • Types: Direct (same-side) and indirect (cross-side) network effects.
    • Critical Mass: Tipping point for widespread adoption.
    • Winner-Take-All Dynamics: Markets where one platform dominates due to strong network effects.
    • Business Strategies:
      • Early user acquisition tactics.
      • Compatibility and standardization decisions.
  • Digital Economy Business Models
    • Freemium Models: Basic services for free, premium features for a fee.
    • Data Monetization: Leveraging user data for targeted advertising or insights.
    • Platform Ecosystems: Creating value through third-party complementors.
    • Implications for Competition Policy: Challenges in defining markets and assessing market power.

Auction Theory

  • Auction Formats
    • Open Auctions:
      • English (ascending bid).
      • Dutch (descending bid).
    • Sealed-Bid Auctions:
      • First-price sealed-bid.
      • Second-price sealed-bid (Vickrey auction).
    • Common Value vs. Private Value Auctions: Differences in information structure.
  • Revenue Equivalence Theorem
    • Statement: Under certain conditions, different auction formats yield the same expected revenue.
    • Assumptions and Implications: Importance for auction design.
  • Business and Policy Applications
    • Spectrum Auctions: Allocation of radio frequencies.
    • Treasury Bond Auctions: Government debt issuance.
    • Online Marketplaces: eBay, Google AdWords.
    • Procurement Auctions: Reverse auctions for supply contracts.

Experimental Economics

  • Methodology
    • Laboratory Experiments: Controlled environments to test economic theories.
    • Field Experiments: Real-world settings with randomized interventions.
    • Design Considerations: Incentive compatibility, external validity.
  • Key Findings
    • Ultimatum Game: Deviations from pure self-interest predictions.
    • Public Goods Games: Free-riding and cooperation dynamics.
    • Market Experiments: Testing efficiency of different market mechanisms.
  • Business Applications
    • A/B Testing: Experimental approach to product and website design.
    • Organizational Design: Testing incentive structures and team dynamics.
    • Consumer Behavior: Experimental insights for marketing strategies.

Market Design

  • Principles
    • Thickness: Bringing together a sufficient number of market participants.
    • Congestion Management: Handling large numbers of transactions quickly.
    • Safety: Making it safe to participate with truthful information.
  • Matching Markets
    • Deferred Acceptance Algorithm: Stable matching in two-sided markets.
    • School Choice Systems: Designing efficient and fair school assignment mechanisms.
    • Medical Residency Matching: National Resident Matching Program (NRMP) as an example.
  • Kidney Exchange
    • Paired Donation: Matching incompatible donor-patient pairs.
    • Chain Donations: Initiated by altruistic donors.
    • Algorithmic Approaches: Optimization in kidney exchange programs.
  • Business Applications
    • Labor Market Platforms: Designing efficient job matching systems.
    • Supply Chain Optimization: Applying matching principles to supplier-buyer relationships.
    • Resource Allocation in Organizations: Efficient project or task assignment.

Advanced Econometric Methods

  • Instrumental Variables
    • Concept: Using instruments to address endogeneity issues.
    • Two-Stage Least Squares (2SLS): Implementation and interpretation.
    • Applications: Estimating returns to education, impact of institutions on economic growth.
  • Difference-in-Differences
    • Method: Comparing treatment and control groups before and after an intervention.
    • Parallel Trends Assumption: Key identifying assumption.
    • Applications: Evaluating policy changes, natural experiments in business settings.
  • Regression Discontinuity Design
    • Sharp RD: Treatment assigned based on a cutoff value of a running variable.
    • Fuzzy RD: Probability of treatment changes discontinuously at the cutoff.
    • Applications: Evaluating programs with eligibility thresholds, analyzing close elections.
  • Business Applications
    • Causal Impact of Marketing Campaigns: Using quasi-experimental designs.
    • Program Evaluation: Assessing the effectiveness of business initiatives or policies.
    • Demand Estimation: Addressing endogeneity in price-quantity relationships.

Environmental Economics

  • Externalities
    • Definition: Costs or benefits affecting third parties not involved in the transaction.
    • Types: Positive vs. negative, production vs. consumption externalities.
    • Coase Theorem: Efficiency through bargaining in absence of transaction costs.
  • Pigouvian Taxes
    • Concept: Taxes to internalize negative externalities.
    • Optimal Tax Level: Equating marginal social cost to marginal social benefit.
    • Applications: Carbon taxes, congestion charges.
  • Cap-and-Trade Systems
    • Mechanism: Setting a cap on total emissions and allowing trade of permits.
    • Efficiency Properties: Achieving cost-effective reduction in emissions.
    • Real-World Examples: EU Emissions Trading System, US Acid Rain Program.
  • Business Implications
    • Corporate Social Responsibility: Voluntary internalization of externalities.
    • Green Technology Innovation: Incentives and strategic responses to environmental policies.
    • Risk Management: Adapting to climate change and environmental regulations.

Public Choice Theory

  • Collective Decision Making
    • Voting Paradoxes: Arrow's Impossibility Theorem, Condorcet Paradox.
    • Median Voter Theorem: Convergence to median preference under certain conditions.
    • Logrolling: Vote trading in legislative processes.
  • Bureaucracy and Regulation
    • Principal-Agent Problems in Government: Misaligned incentives between voters, politicians, and bureaucrats.
    • Regulatory Capture: Industries influencing their regulators.
    • Rent-Seeking Behavior: Efforts to capture economic rent through political processes.
  • Business Strategy in Political Environments
    • Lobbying Strategies: Influencing policy outcomes.
    • Regulatory Compliance: Optimizing responses to regulatory frameworks.
    • Political Risk Assessment: Evaluating policy uncertainty in business decisions.

Mechanism Design

  • Principles
    • Incentive Compatibility: Designing mechanisms where truthful revelation is optimal.
    • Individual Rationality: Ensuring voluntary participation.
    • Budget Balance: Mechanisms that don't require external subsidies.
  • Contract Theory
    • Optimal Contracting: Designing contracts to align incentives.
    • Incomplete Contracts: Dealing with unforeseen contingencies.
    • Relational Contracts: Self-enforcing agreements in repeated interactions.
  • Organizational Design Applications
    • Compensation Structures: Designing incentive-compatible pay schemes.
    • Team Production: Addressing free-riding in collaborative environments.
    • Information Systems: Designing mechanisms for efficient information sharing within organizations.
